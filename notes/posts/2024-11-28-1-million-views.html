# 1 million page views ## November 28, 2024 ###### writing I don't regularly check historic blog stats so I was delighted to notice that this site has recently passed 1M page views. And since Murat [wrote](https://muratbuffalo.blogspot.com/2017/02/1-million-pageviews.html) about his 1M page view accomplishment at the time, I felt compelled to now too. ![/assets/1m-page-views.png](/assets/1m-page-views.png) I started regularly blogging in 2018. For some reason I decided to write a blog post every month. And while I have definitely skipped a month or two here or there, on average I've written 2 posts per month. ### Evolution In the beginning I talked more about little interpreters in JavaScript and about Scheme. I was into functional programming. Over time I moved into little emulators and bytecode VMs. And for the last four years I became obsessed with databases and distributed systems. I have almost always written about little projects to teach myself a concept. Writing a [bytecode VM in Rust](https://notes.eatonphil.com/lua-in-rust.html), [emulating a subset of x86 in Go](https://notes.eatonphil.com/emulating-amd64-starting-with-elf.html), [implementing Raft in Go](https://notes.eatonphil.com/2023-05-25-raft.html), [implementing MVCC isolation levels in Go](https://notes.eatonphil.com/2024-05-16-mvcc.html), and so on. I was motivated to write minimal implementations and walk through the code in its entirety because so many times when I tried to learn a concept I would find blog posts with only partial code. They'd link to a github repo that by the time I got to the post, the repo had evolved significantly beyond what was described in the post. And the repo code would now be too complex for me to follow. Even today there is not a single post on implementing TCP/IP from scratch that walks through entirely working code. (Please, someone write this.) I have also had a blast writing survey posts such as [how various databases execute expressions](https://notes.eatonphil.com/2023-09-21-how-do-databases-execute-expressions.html), [analyzing non-V8 JavaScript implementations](https://notes.eatonphil.com/javascript-implementations.html), [how various programming language implementations parse code](https://notes.eatonphil.com/parser-generators-vs-handwritten-parsers-survey-2021.html), and [how various database systems build on top of key-value databases](https://notes.eatonphil.com/whats-the-big-deal-about-key-value-databases.html). The latter two posts have even been cited each in a research paper ([here](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2208.08235) and [here](https://www.usenix.org/system/files/atc23-kaufman.pdf)). ### Editing In terms of quality, my single greatest trick is to read the post out loud. Multiple times. Notice parts that are awkward or unclear and rewrite them. My second greatest trick is to ask friends for review. Some posts like [an intuition for distributed consensus](https://notes.eatonphil.com/2024-02-08-an-intuition-for-distributed-consensus-in-oltp-systems.html) and [a write-ahead log is not a universal part of durability](https://notes.eatonphil.com/2024-07-01-a-write-ahead-log-is-not-a-universal-part-of-durability.html) would simply not have been correct or credible without my fantastic reviewers. And I'm proud to have [played that part](https://eatonphil.com/editor.html) a few times in turn. We also have a fantastic #writing-and-drafts channel on the [Software Internals Discord](https://eatonphil.com/discord.html) where folks (myself occasionally included) come for post review. ### Context I've lost count of the total number of times that these posts have been on the front page of Hacker News or that a tweet announcing a post has reached triple digits likes. I think I've had 9 posts on the front of HN this year. I do know that my single best year for HN was 12 months between 2022-2023 where 20 of my posts or projects were on the front page. Every time a post does well there's a part of me that worries that I've peaked. But the way to deal with this has been to ignore that little voice and to just keep learning new things. I haven't stopped finding things confusing yet, and [confusion is a great muse](https://notes.eatonphil.com/2024-06-14-confusion-is-a-muse.html). And also to, like, go out and meet friends for dinner, [run](https://nycsystems.xyz/) [meetups](https://eatonphil.com/nyc-systems-coffee-club.html), [chat](https://eatonphil.com/chat.html) with you fascinating internet strangers, play volleyball, and so on. It's always been about [cultivating healthy obsessions](https://notes.eatonphil.com/2024-08-24-obsession.html). ### Benediction In parting, I'll remind you: - [It is definitely worth writing about](https://notes.eatonphil.com/is-it-worth-writing-about.html), whatever "it" is - [You're not writing enough](https://twitter.com/eatonphil/status/1854965419745972394) - And [some ideas for posts I want to hear about if you write about them](https://eatonphil.com/call-for-posts.html)
